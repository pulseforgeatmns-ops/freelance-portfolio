The Part of Her Business That Ran Without Her Knowing It Was Broken

A review request that never goes out is invisible. Nobody complains. No alarm goes off. The business just quietly loses the chance to build credibility it already earned.

That's exactly what was happening for one of our clients, a home services company operating out of Goffstown that does work across the greater Manchester, NH area. She had a solid reputation, a steady stream of jobs, and almost no online reviews to show for it. Not because clients were unhappy. Because nobody was asking.

What the Gap Actually Looked Like

Her process after completing a job was manual from start to finish. She'd wrap up, invoice the client, maybe send a thank-you text if she remembered, and move on to the next thing. The review request? It lived in her head as something she intended to do later.

Later usually didn't come. She was already on the next job.

When we mapped out her post-job workflow, the gap wasn't a mystery. There was no trigger. Nothing that said "job complete" and automatically kicked off a follow-up sequence. The system wasn't broken because something failed. It was broken because it didn't exist.

What We Built Instead

The fix wasn't complicated, but it had to be precise. We built a short automated sequence that fired within an hour of a job being marked complete. The first message was a simple, personal-sounding thank-you. The second, sent two days later, included a direct link to leave a review on Google.

No logins. No copy-pasting. No relying on her remembering.

Within six weeks, she had more Google reviews than she'd collected in the previous two years. Her profile went from something people skipped past to something that actually reinforced the referrals she was already getting. A few of those reviews came from clients she'd worked with twelve months earlier who just hadn't been asked until now.

That last detail matters. The system didn't just catch new jobs. The early part of the sequence included a short re-engagement message to past clients she hadn't followed up with. Manchester, NH has a tight service community, and those older clients were still active. Some of them left reviews the same day they heard from her.

The Structural Lesson Here

What this owner had wasn't a marketing problem. She had a handoff problem. The work was good. The clients were satisfied. The system just had no mechanism to convert that goodwill into something visible.

Fixing it didn't require a bigger ad budget or a new platform. It required identifying the exact moment where the process stalled and building something that moved automatically through that moment without her.

That's the pattern we see repeatedly across service businesses in the area. The reputation is already there. The automation is just the thing that captures it before it slips through.

We've built this for restaurants, cleaners, contractors, and salons across NH. The playbook is the same. The results aren't.
