The 3 Emails You Should Be Sending Every Month (But Probably Aren't)

Here at Pulseforge, we work with small business owners across Manchester, NH who are great at what they do but stretched thin when it comes to staying in front of their customers. One of the first things we look at when we start working with someone is their email rhythm. Not their social media. Not their ads. Their email. Because most of the time, there are three simple messages sitting on the table that nobody is sending.

The "You Haven't Heard From Us In A While" Email

This one targets people who inquired, bought once, or signed up for something and then went quiet. A short, direct message that says you noticed they haven't been around and wanted to check in. No pressure, no pitch. Just a reminder that you exist and you remember them. This email alone brings people back. Set it to go out automatically after 60 days of inactivity and forget about it. It works while you sleep.

The "Here's Something Useful" Email

This is not a promotion. It is a tip, a reminder, a local heads up, something genuinely helpful to the kind of person who buys from you. A plumber in Bedford might send a reminder about winterizing pipes in October. A bookkeeper serving small businesses in Manchester, NH might send a one paragraph note about a tax deadline that snuck up on people last year. The goal is to show up in the inbox as someone worth hearing from, not just someone asking for money. Send one of these per month and your open rates will tell the story.

The "We'd Love A Review" Email

Timing is everything here. This message goes out a few days after someone completes a purchase or service, when the experience is still fresh. Keep it short. One sentence asking how things went, one link to leave a review. That is it. Businesses in the Manchester area that automate this step consistently outpace competitors on Google and Yelp simply because they ask at the right moment every single time instead of remembering to do it manually when they think of it.

None of these emails are complicated to write. What makes them effective is consistency, and that is exactly where manual sending falls apart. Life gets busy, follow up slips, and the people who could have come back simply drift away.
