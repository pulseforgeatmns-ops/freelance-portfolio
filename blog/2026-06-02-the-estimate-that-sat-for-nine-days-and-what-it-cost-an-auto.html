The Estimate That Sat for Nine Days (And What It Cost an Auto Shop to Find Out)

Nine days is how long one follow-up email sat unsent for an auto shop we work with in Manchester, NH. Not because anyone forgot. Because nobody owned it.

The shop does honest work. Skilled technicians, transparent pricing, repeat customers who trust them. But between the phone calls, the jobs on the lift, and the end-of-day closeouts, there was a gap nobody had mapped. Customers who asked for estimates and didn't book weren't getting any follow-up at all. The shop assumed they'd call back if they were interested. Some did. Most didn't.

Here's what that gap actually looked like when we audited it.

The Leak Nobody Was Tracking

The shop wasn't struggling with leads or reputation. Their problem was a handoff that didn't exist. An estimate would go out by phone or email, the customer would say they'd think about it, and that was the end of the trail. No reminder. No check-in. No way to know if the customer booked somewhere else or just needed one more nudge.

When we mapped out their estimate-to-booked ratio over 60 days, the number was lower than anyone expected. Not because their prices were off or their mechanics weren't trusted, but because there was no system holding the gap between "sent estimate" and "confirmed appointment." The estimates that converted were the ones where the service advisor happened to remember to call back. That's not a process. That's luck.

For a shop running four bays in a competitive market like Manchester, that gap translates fast. We're talking multiple unboooked jobs per week that had already cleared the hardest part, which is getting a customer interested enough to ask for a quote.

What the Fix Actually Looked Like

We built a follow-up sequence that triggered automatically the moment an estimate was sent. No clicks required from the service desk. If the customer didn't respond within 48 hours, they got a short, plain-language message from the shop. Not a marketing email. Something that sounded like a service advisor checking in.

If they still hadn't booked after day five, a second message went out with a direct link to the shop's online booking page. If they booked at any point, the sequence stopped. No over-messaging, no awkward double contact.

In the first 30 days after launch, the shop recovered 11 jobs that had gone quiet after the estimate stage. Those weren't new leads. They were already in the pipeline. The system just stopped letting them expire.

Why This Pattern Shows Up in Almost Every Trade Business

It's not unique to auto repair. Flooring contractors, HVAC companies, and plumbers across the Manchester area run into the same issue. The leads they generate aren't the bottleneck. It's the handoff between "expressed interest" and "confirmed job" where the revenue quietly disappears.

The fix is almost always the same: a lightweight automation that holds the gap without requiring anyone to remember to act. No new software to learn. No extra hire. Just a sequence that runs in the background and catches what the team doesn't have bandwidth to chase.

That's the result this shop got. Not from changing what they sold or how they priced it. From finally closing the loop on work they'd already done to bring someone to the door.
