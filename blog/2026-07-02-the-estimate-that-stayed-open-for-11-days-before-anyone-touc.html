The Estimate That Stayed Open for 11 Days Before Anyone Touched It

An auto shop running at full capacity can still be quietly bleeding revenue, and the bleed usually happens in the gap between "sent the estimate" and "heard back from the customer."

That's exactly what we found when an independent auto repair shop in Providence, RI came to us earlier this year. The owner was booked out, his techs were busy, and from the outside, the business looked healthy. But when we dug into his follow-up process, we found 23 open estimates sitting in his inbox, some of them more than a week old, with zero contact made after the initial quote went out.

He wasn't ignoring them. He just didn't have a system for chasing them down, and every day that passed made it less likely those customers would say yes.

Where the Revenue Was Going

The average estimate this shop sends out runs between $300 and $900. With 23 open quotes and no follow-up, the owner was sitting on somewhere between $6,900 and $20,000 in potential revenue that hadn't been touched.

The reason wasn't neglect. It was that follow-up was a manual task sitting at the bottom of a very full day. By the time he got to it, he was either too tired or two days late, and neither of those outcomes closed jobs.

The pattern isn't unique to Providence, RI or to auto repair. Any service business that sends estimates and waits for a reply is running a system built on hope.

What the Automated Follow-Up Actually Looked Like

We built a simple follow-up sequence triggered the moment an estimate was sent. If the customer didn't respond within 48 hours, they got a text. Friendly, short, direct. If they still hadn't responded by day four, they got a second one with a soft reminder about part availability and scheduling.

No pressure. No spam. Just the kind of follow-up a great service advisor would do if they had unlimited time, which nobody does.

Within the first 30 days, the shop recovered 6 jobs from estimates that had already gone cold. That's not 6 new customers. That's 6 people who already said they wanted the work done, who just needed someone to follow up with them at the right moment.

The owner told us the one that surprised him most was an estimate that had been sitting open for 11 days. The customer responded to the automated text within 20 minutes and booked the job the same afternoon. He would never have followed up on that one manually.

What the Actual Takeaway Is

If you're running a service business and you send any kind of quote, estimate, or proposal, the question worth asking isn't "do I have a marketing problem?" It's "what happens to that quote after it leaves my hands?"

For most shops, the honest answer is: not much. And that gap is where money disappears quietly, without showing up on any report.
