The Follow-Up Email You Never Sent Is Costing You Customers

You met someone at a Chamber event in Manchester last Tuesday. They seemed interested. You said you'd send them something. And then Wednesday happened, and Thursday, and now it's been two weeks and that conversation is dead in the water.

This is not a discipline problem. It is a capacity problem.

At Pulseforge, we work with small business owners across Manchester, NH who are genuinely good at what they do but keep losing ground on the back end because there are only so many hours in a day. The follow-up, the check-in, the "just wanted to circle back" message are exactly the tasks that feel small until you realize how many of them never happen.

What Automated Outreach Actually Looks Like in Practice

Here is the practical version, not the sales pitch version.

You set up a sequence once. A new lead comes in through your website, your Google profile, or a form you shared at an event. Within minutes, they get a personal-feeling email from you. Two days later, if they have not responded, they get a short follow-up. A week after that, one more. You never touch any of it.

The messages are written in your voice, reference your specific service, and stop automatically the moment someone replies or books. Nothing robotic. Nothing that reads like a mass blast. Just consistent, timely outreach that would have fallen through the cracks otherwise.

Three Sequences Worth Setting Up First

If you are starting from scratch, these are the ones that pay off fastest.

New lead follow-up: The message you would send the same day if you had time. Most businesses wait 48 hours or longer. Speed here matters more than most people realize.

Inactive customer re-engagement: Anyone who used your service more than 90 days ago and has not been back. A simple "we have not seen you in a while" message with a specific reason to return works better than a discount blast.

Review request: Sent automatically two to three days after a job is complete or a service is delivered. Southern New Hampshire has no shortage of local competitors. Reviews are how you stand out on searches without spending anything on ads.

None of these require technical skills to set up. They require about an hour of thinking through what you would actually want to say, and then letting the system handle the rest.
