The Auto Shop That Lost $4,200 in Declined Jobs — Then Got It Back Without Hiring Anyone

Nine declined jobs in a single month sounds like a slow month. For a shop in Manchester that runs six bays and does everything from oil changes to suspension work, it was actually a follow-through problem disguised as a slow month.

Here's what was happening. A customer would come in, get a recommended repair they weren't prepared for that day, decline it, and leave. The advisor would write a note. Sometimes that note turned into a phone call two weeks later. Mostly it didn't. Life got busy, the bay got full, and the declined job quietly disappeared into the history of the day.

When we audited the previous 90 days of records with this shop, the number that came back was $4,200 in unrecovered declined service revenue. Not leads that never came in. Jobs that had already walked through the door and been seen by a technician.

Where the Leak Was

The problem wasn't the advisors. It wasn't the pricing. It was the gap between "customer said not today" and "someone followed up in a useful window."

The shop had no consistent system for flagging declined work, timing a follow-up, and sending that follow-up without requiring the service advisor to remember to do it. It was a handoff problem. The moment the customer left without booking, the job existed in a note that nobody had a system to act on.

This is common in service businesses across Manchester and Southern NH. The front-end work is solid. The back-end follow-through depends entirely on how much bandwidth the owner or lead advisor has on a given day. On a heavy Tuesday in a six-bay shop, that bandwidth is usually near zero.

What the System Actually Did

We built an automated follow-up sequence tied to their declined service records. When a job got flagged as declined in their system, a sequence triggered automatically. The first message went out at 48 hours. A second at 12 days. A third at 30 days if there had been no response.

Each message referenced the specific service that had been recommended. Not a generic "we haven't seen you in a while" message. A specific, plain-language note about the job their technician had flagged, why it mattered, and an easy way to get back on the schedule.

In the first 60 days, six of those dormant declined jobs converted. Two came back within the first week of the sequence going live. The shop didn't add staff, didn't change pricing, and didn't run a single promotion. They recovered revenue that had already been earned at the diagnostic level and just needed a consistent nudge to close.

The owner's comment after the first month was straightforward: "We were just forgetting to ask."

That's the honest version of what automation does at its best. It doesn't manufacture interest that isn't there. It shows up consistently in the moments where a human would have moved on to the next task.

For a service business in Manchester running on tight margins and full bays, the difference between a $2,000 month and a $6,000 month isn't always new customers. Sometimes it's the ones who already said "maybe later" and never heard back.

We've built this for restaurants, cleaners, contractors, and salons across NH. The playbook is the same. The results aren't.
